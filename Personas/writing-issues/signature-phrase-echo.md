# Issue: Signature-phrase echo (origin tagline + "too much")

**Status:** OPEN (recurring — watch every recipe entry AND every memorial/dedication). **REC-001 instances: CLEARED at v10** (Giselle's "all of this" → "the whole thing"; only Athena's load-bearing "started all of this" + the headline remain). **About-page memorials: OPEN (new instance, 2026-06-22)** — origin claim fires 3x in Athena's opening (see below). The pattern stays open as a series-level watch-item.
**Severity:** Low per instance, but it keeps recurring, which is why it's tracked.
**Scope:** Recipe story headnotes + any per-recipe launch copy (newsletter, social, meta) + the About-page memorials/dedications. First observed on REC-001 French Silk Pie across draft versions 2–8; recurs in `Personas/About-Page-Memorials-and-Dedication-DRAFT.md`.

## What it is

A small set of brand-signature phrases get reused so often, across so many surfaces, that an attentive reader notices the repetition. The story's emotional hooks ("the pie that started everything / all of this / whole world" and "too much / a bit much") are strong enough that the temptation is to restate them — and over several edit passes they kept multiplying and kept needing manual de-duplication.

## Evidence (REC-001)

- **Origin tagline.** At its peak the "started everything / started all of this / started it all" phrasing appeared ~5x across story close, recipe one-liner, meta description, and newsletter subject. Trimmed repeatedly (v3 "de-duplicated origin phrasing"; v6 "broke the 3x 'started everything' echo"). v8 still carries the story headline "The pie that started everything" plus Athena's "the one who started all of this" — acceptable (two, different roles), but it re-grew between passes.
- **"all of this" residue (v8).** Athena: "the one who started all of this" (para 3); Giselle: "tell you she could do all of this better" (para 4) — two in close range.
- **"too much / a bit much."** Intentional brand hook, but appears in the story (the "bit rich/bit much" beat + the close) and any marketing copy; combined with the origin tagline it stacks up.

## Evidence (About-page memorials, 2026-06-22)

- **Origin claim, 3x in ~3 sentences (Athena).** The section intro closes "We start with **the one who started it all**." Athena's first sentence then says she's "**the reason any of this exists**," and the next sentence opens "**Before there was a Giselle, before there was a table, there was Athena**." Three statements of the same "this is where it all began" idea, stacked. The anaphora line ("Before there was…") is the strongest; "the reason any of this exists" is the cuttable middle restatement. "The one who started it all" can stay — it's the section hand-off, a different job.
- **"too much" hook (Giselle dedication):** "to be 'too much' everywhere but home." Single, load-bearing use here — this is the *right* way to spend the hook (once, with conviction). Noted only to confirm it isn't also restated in the eventual About-page launch copy.

## Why it matters

Recycled taglines are a recognizable AI/repetition tell and they dull the phrase that should land hardest. The "too much" reframe is the emotional core; it's most powerful stated once with conviction, not used as a refrain.

## Direction for future entries

- Pick ONE hero phrasing per concept per surface. Let the headline own the origin tagline; vary the in-prose and marketing restatements.
- Keep load-bearing uses (e.g., Athena = "the one who started all of this," which sets up the matriarch) and cut the incidental echoes.
- Add to the pre-publish checklist: grep the entry + its launch copy for the origin tagline and the "too much" hook; confirm each appears intentionally, not by habit.
