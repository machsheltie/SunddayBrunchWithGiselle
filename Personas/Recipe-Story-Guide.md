# Recipe Story Guide — Writing the Headnote

*How to write the personal story that opens every recipe, so each one sounds like Stacey and pulls the reader into our world. Part of the Sunday Brunch recipe format (see `Recipe-Page-Sheltie-Segments-Guide.md` and `Recipe-Writing-Guide.md`).*

---

## What the story is for

The story is the headnote — the part above the recipe where I (Stacey) tell you why this dish matters. It has three jobs, in order:

1. **Grab the reader in the first line.** They decide whether to keep reading almost immediately.
2. **Sell the world.** By the end, a brand-new visitor should understand what this place is — the cozy Sunday table, the four opinionated Shelties, the "being a little too much is exactly enough" feeling — without me ever explaining it like a mission statement.
3. **Make them want to come back.** Leave them feeling invited in, not sold to.

Our north star for craft is Smitten Kitchen (Deb Perelman). Our voice is mine, with Giselle interrupting.

---

## The Smitten Kitchen principles we steal

- **Write how you talk.** Like a friend on the phone with the long cord, talking while she cooks. If you wouldn't say it out loud, cut it.
- **Take the static out.** Anticipate the thing the reader will worry about and answer it before they panic ("it'll look broken at minute three — keep going"). This is the single most useful move and it's very us.
- **Earn the length.** A headnote can be a few paragraphs, but every sentence has to pull its weight. Long is fine; padded is not.
- **Be actually funny and actually specific.** Real domestic detail (the dogs, the kitchen, the mess) beats pretty generalities.
- **Don't perform.** Natural, not writerly. Never write like I think I'm a better baker than the reader — I'm right there in the mess with them.

---

## Structure (a flexible template, not a formula)

1. **Hook** — one strong line. A confession, a blunt opinion, or a small scene. No warm-up, no "I should tell you…"
2. **The personal thread** — the specific memory, person, or moment this dish is tied to. Specific beats sweet.
3. **A world beat** — somewhere in the middle, let the Shelties, the Sunday-table feeling, or the "too much = just enough" ethos show up naturally.
4. **The useful turn** — often a take-the-static-out moment: what made this hard, what you learned, what the reader should expect. Bridges story to recipe.
5. **The close** — invite them in and point forward (this is the first of many Sundays; pull up a chair).

Giselle interrupts 2–3 times, set off clearly, with one sharp loving jab each. She is the only dog in the story; the others wait for their boxes.

---

## Length

- **Standard:** 4–6 short paragraphs, about 250–400 words.
- **Everyday/quick recipes:** 2–3 short paragraphs is plenty — don't force a saga onto a weeknight muffin.
- **Marquee/signature recipes (like a first recipe or a holiday showstopper):** the full 5–6 paragraphs, because these are the ones that recruit new readers.
- Giselle's interjections: 1–2 sentences each.

---

## Voice kit (Stacey)

- Talks to one friend: "Hi friend," "save me a slice," "I'm so glad you're here."
- Self-deprecating tangents that I catch myself in.
- Gentle asides to the dogs mid-thought.
- Nostalgia (80s/90s kid), kitchen-as-safe-space, glitter-and-magic streak.
- **Show the anxiety, don't label it.** "Going quiet in exactly the moments I wanted to be charming" beats "I'm neurodivergent and anxious."

For Giselle's voice in the story, see the Sheltie segments guide.

---

## Hard boundaries (these are mine alone)

Real memories presented as fact about real people, the real dogs, family, grief, identity, or anything vulnerable are **human-voice-only** — they're written by me and stay true to what actually happened, or stay clearly impressionistic rather than inventing hard biographical claims. The dogs never narrate a real memory. When a story touches something tender, keep it honest and keep it mine. (See the character canon's human-voice-only list.)

## AI-tell checklist (run before publishing)

- [ ] Opening is a real hook, not a setup frame ("I should tell you up front…").
- [ ] Anxiety/feelings are *shown* through specifics, not named like keywords.
- [ ] No more than two "like a ___" similes close together.
- [ ] Em dashes are occasional, not a tic. (My natural voice uses ellipses and dashes — a few is me, a dozen is a robot.)
- [ ] No moralizing sermon or tidy life-lesson wrap-up. Let Giselle land the emotional beat instead.
- [ ] Rhythm varies — some short punchy sentences, some that wander.
- [ ] Specific over vague everywhere (named feelings, real objects, actual moments).
- [ ] None of these words: "genuinely," "honestly," "testament," "elevate," "nestled," "boasts," "vibrant," "a perfect blend of."
- [ ] Read it out loud. If a sentence doesn't sound like me talking, fix it.

## Story starters (for when the blank page is mean)

Pick one and pull the thread:

- Who first put this in front of me, and what was happening that day?
- The version that went wrong before this one went right.
- Why *this* take on it — what I changed and why I couldn't leave it alone.
- The moment I realized this dish was a keeper.
- Who I make it for now, and what their face does.

## Example (French Silk Pie)

Hook: *"The first French Silk Pie I ever made was a bribe."* From there: the loud-Thanksgiving "I'll bake my way in" story → the "it's a bit rich / you're a bit much" beat → the reframe (rich isn't a flaw) → a close that names the podcast, the site, and the four Shelties and invites the reader to pull up a chair.
