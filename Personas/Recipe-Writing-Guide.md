# Recipe Writing Guide — The Recipe Itself

*How to write the recipe portion of every page so a casual baker can read it, understand it, and pull it off on the first try. The story has its own guide (`Recipe-Story-Guide.md`); the Sheltie boxes have theirs (`Recipe-Page-Sheltie-Segments-Guide.md`). This one is the recipe.*

---

## The one goal

A nervous, first-time baker should be able to follow this and succeed. Everything below serves that. The voice is mine — warm, clear, encouraging — but in the recipe, clarity always wins over charm. Be the calm friend standing next to them at the counter.

---

## The faithfulness rule (important)

When I hand over an old or sourced recipe to rewrite, the job is **voice and clarity, not reformulation.** Keep every amount, time, temperature, and step exactly as given unless I explicitly approve a change. We can clarify, reorder for readability, and add helpful cues — but we don't quietly change quantities or method. The recipe is mine to change; the writeup is what we're polishing.

---

## Page map for the recipe portion (top to bottom)

1. **Title** + a one-line description in my voice (what it is, why you want it).
2. **Stats line:** active time · chill/rest · total · makes · difficulty.
3. **Read This First** — the 2–3 things that decide success.
4. **Equipment.**
5. **Ingredients**, grouped by component.
6. *(🐾 Havok's Kitchen Recon slots in here — see segments guide.)*
7. **Instructions**, numbered.
8. *(🐾 Tiana's Tasting Notes after the last step.)*
9. **Notes & troubleshooting** — always at the bottom, where readers expect it. *(🐾 Phaedra's Porch Light lives in here.)*

---

## Stats line — be honest

Give real numbers a beginner can plan around. If the original mislabels something, fix the framing: a "2-minute cook time" that's really just melting chocolate becomes honest active/chill/total time. Always surface long chill or rest times up front — that's the thing that ruins a same-day plan.

---

## Read This First — take the static out

A short box at the very top with the 2–3 make-or-break items, so nobody learns them too late. This is where any **safety note** goes, in my voice (see Safety below). Think: the special ingredient they need to buy ahead, the equipment that really matters, the technique people most often rush. Two to four short items, no more.

Add a one-line note here that the Shelties pop in along the way and their boxes are skippable — it sets the format and protects readability.

---

## Ingredients

- **Group by component** ("For the filling," "For the topping") in the order they're used.
- **US volume first, metric weight in parentheses** for anything where weight matters — chocolate, butter, sugar, flour, cream. Use standard, correct conversions; double-check them.
- **State the prep and temperature in the ingredient line:** "softened to room temperature," "cold," "sifted," "room temperature." Temperature is half the battle in a lot of our recipes, so say it here *and* in the step.
- Flag anything with a safety or allergen note inline, pointing to Read This First or Notes.

---

## Instructions

- **Numbered, one main action per step.** A short bold lead label on each step helps scanning ("**Beat the butter and sugar.**").
- **Plain, direct language.** Short sentences. Active voice. No fussy vocabulary.
- **Give a "what you're watching for" cue** at every step where a beginner would be unsure: what it should look, feel, or sound like, and how to know it's ready ("pale and fluffy," "holds a stiff peak," "rub it between two fingers — no sugar grains"). This is the most important readability upgrade we make.
- **Call out the moment things look wrong but aren't** ("around minute three it'll seem broken — keep going"). Borrowed straight from Smitten Kitchen.
- **Set warnings and recovery apart** from the action so they're not missed, but never bury a real safety step inside a chatty aside.
- **One or two gentle asides are welcome** ("set a timer, put on a podcast — ahem"), but keep them out of the actual measurements and timings. The steps stay clean.
- **Times and temperatures are exact.** Give a thermometer target where food safety depends on it.

---

## Safety — always my voice, always clear

Food safety, raw-egg and temperature calls, and allergens are human-voice-only. They go up top in Read This First (not buried in Notes), stated plainly, with a backup method when one exists (for example, the cooked-egg method for anyone who can't get pasteurized eggs). A Sheltie can explain *why* the safe method works (that's Phaedra), but the instruction itself is always mine. Never soften or joke a safety line.

---

## Notes & troubleshooting (bottom of the page)

This is the reference shelf, and it lives at the bottom of every entry. Include what applies:

- **Safety/alternative method** (e.g., cooked-egg version) — my voice.
- **🐾 Phaedra's Porch Light** — why it works + the symptom → cause → fix list.
- **Make ahead** — what can be done in advance and how far.
- **Storage** — how and how long; fridge/freezer.
- **Freezing / reheating / transport / gifting** — where relevant.

---

## Formatting

- Bold and grouped lists are good *here* — a recipe is meant to be scanned, so structure earns its keep. (This is the opposite of the story, which is prose.)
- Don't bold random phrases mid-sentence; bold is for step labels and section headers only.
- Keep metric conversions in parentheses, units consistent, fractions clean (½, ⅔).
- Straight quotes, not curly. No emoji except the 🐾 on Sheltie segment headers.

---

## Per-recipe checklist (run before publishing)

- [ ] Title + one-line description in my voice.
- [ ] Honest stats line; long chill/rest time surfaced up top.
- [ ] Read This First covers the 2–3 success keys + any safety note (my voice).
- [ ] Ingredients grouped, with weights and prep/temperature states.
- [ ] Every tricky step has a "what you're watching for" cue.
- [ ] "Looks wrong but isn't" moments called out.
- [ ] Safety stated plainly up top, with a backup method if one exists.
- [ ] Notes at the bottom: alternative method, make-ahead, storage.
- [ ] All four Sheltie boxes present, in place, and short (see segments guide).
- [ ] Amounts/times/method unchanged from the approved recipe.
- [ ] Read the steps as if I've never made it — could I succeed?
