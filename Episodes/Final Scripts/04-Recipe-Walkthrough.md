# Sunday Brunch With Giselle — Episode 1
## Segment 4 — Recipe Walkthrough: French Silk Pie
*"Let's Bake It Together." Final script. Cast: Stacey, Giselle.*

---

[Sound: something fast and a little too loud from the Echo Dot — then nudged down a notch. A bowl set on the counter. The unhurried click of nails crossing tile, proprietary, in no particular rush.]

**STACEY:** So — let me teach you how to make the pie that started all of this.

Here's the first thing, and it's the thing nobody tells you up front: this recipe is almost entirely waiting. If you came for a fast dessert — I love you, truly, go make brownies, we'll be right here when you get back. Still with me? Good.

**GISELLE:** [from the cushion, not bothering to rise] A rare moment of honesty. Most people promise their listeners ease. You are promising them labor.

**STACEY:** I'm promising them silk.

**STACEY:** Okay. Before you touch anything — your butter and your eggs have to be room temperature. Not cold, not melty. Room temp. Which means you took them out an hour ago. So if you're hearing this and your butter is still in the fridge —

**GISELLE:** Postpone. Silk does not negotiate with cold butter.

**STACEY:** She's not being dramatic, which — for the record — is rare. Cold butter can't hold the air you're about to beat into it, and the air *is* the pie. Press a finger into it. It should give when you press it, like memory foam. If it pushes back, it isn't ready yet.

**STACEY:** And the crust. I'm using store-bought. There. On the record.

**GISELLE:** [a single, wounded sigh] An offense to my very being.

**STACEY:** It's a Tuesday, Giselle.

**GISELLE:** It is Sunday.

**STACEY:** …It's emotionally a Tuesday. Listen — buy the good one. I get the Marie Callender's deep-dish out of the freezer aisle. Bake it, cool it all the way, feel zero guilt. The filling is where the work goes. Nobody at your table is going to interrogate the crust.

**GISELLE:** And if they do, they are not your people.

**STACEY:** And Giselle will deal with them.

**GISELLE:** Gladly.

**STACEY:** Now — melt your chocolate. Unsweetened. The exact amounts are all on the site, so you're not pausing this with chocolate on your hands. Microwave it in short bursts, stirring between each. Or use a double boiler if you don't trust yourself, and I say that with love, because *I* do not trust myself.

**STACEY:** The one thing that ruins it here is seizing. If your smooth chocolate suddenly goes grainy and stiff on you — that's seized. Too much heat, or a single drop of water got in.

**GISELLE:** The chocolate flinched. You startled it, and it will not forgive you. Begin again.

**STACEY:** …That's the clearest anyone's ever explained it to me. It flinches. So keep it gentle, keep it just-barely-melted, and the second it's smooth enough to draw a clean line through with your spoon — stop. Set it aside to cool. Warm chocolate plus butter is chocolate soup, and we are not making soup.

**STACEY:** Okay. The part your arms will remember. You want a stand mixer for this. I tried it once with a hand mixer, and my right shoulder still brings it up in arguments.

**GISELLE:** I tried to warn you. I barked through the entire ordeal — start to finish. You called it "unhelpful." It was the most useful thing said in this kitchen all day.

**STACEY:** Constructive. Sure. Cream your butter with the sugar. And here's the one ingredient I'll actually fight you on: ultrafine sugar. Not regular.

**GISELLE:** Tell them what you did.

**STACEY:** [sigh] I made it with regular sugar three times in one night. Grainier every time. Swearing at — to be clear — completely innocent ingredients. Before I learned you can pulse regular sugar in the food processor to fake ultrafine. Or just buy ultrafine and skip the whole breakdown. Both kinds of breakdown.

**GISELLE:** She learned. Eventually. The way water eventually wears down stone.

**STACEY:** I'll take it — erosion's still progress. Beat the butter and sugar a full three minutes. You'll *hear* it change — it starts heavy, almost a slap, and goes lighter, quieter, until it looks pale and cloud-ish, like it could float off the beater. That's the air. That's what you want.

**GISELLE:** And this is where most people stop. They see "fluffy," they decide "close enough," and they spend the rest of their lives making *adequate* desserts. Three minutes. Set a timer. Do not trust your optimism.

**STACEY:** She's right — I always think I'm there at ninety seconds. I'm never there at ninety seconds.

**STACEY:** Then drizzle the cooled chocolate in slow. No rush — it's a courtship, not a race.

**GISELLE:** And like any courtship: rush it, and it curdles.

**STACEY:** [laughing] Okay, that's going on a tea towel. Then the real vanilla. The real stuff. I'm not going to lecture you —

**GISELLE:** I will. Imitation vanilla is a *rumor* of vanilla. A vanilla-scented apology.

**STACEY:** …Yeah. Use the real stuff. It hits the warm butter and for about four seconds the whole kitchen smells like a bakery you'd drive across town for. That's the part I'm in it for.

**STACEY:** Now. The eggs. Four of them, pasteurized — because they stay raw in this pie, and pasteurized means safe. One at a time. And here's where I have to be honest with you, the way I wish someone had been honest with me: you beat a full five minutes after each egg. Five. Times four.

**GISELLE:** [theatrical] Twenty minutes. Say your goodbyes. Cancel your afternoon.

**STACEY:** And somewhere in that first egg — around minute three — you'll look down and the whole thing is going to look *broken.* Curdled. Grainy. Wrong. You'll be certain you've wasted two sticks of good butter.

**GISELLE:** You have not. Keep going.

**STACEY:** Keep going. This is the exact spot everyone quits — and it's the only part that matters. Scrape the bowl between eggs, the bottom too, not just the sides. And slowly, egg by egg, that broken-looking mess starts to pull itself back together. It lightens. It lifts — the color goes soft on you. You'll hear the mixer change too — less of a struggle, more of a hum.

[Sound: a single click of nails on tile. Then a flurry of them. A low grumble starting up somewhere down near the floor.]

**STACEY:** …And that'll be my supervision arriving. Giselle cannot physically watch me do this unattended.

**GISELLE:** [a low rowl climbing up under the words] You are scraping the *sides.* You are not scraping the *bottom.* The bottom, human. I can see it from down here. I have been able to see it for some time.

**STACEY:** [scraping] Bottom too. Yes. [aside, fond] This is the whole routine — the nails, the grumble, a paw to the shin, the growl that turns into a *speech* — and it does not stop until the kitchen bends to her will.

**GISELLE:** It is not a growl. It is escalating executive feedback. [one sharp, decisive bark] …There. Now you're listening.

[Sound: the mixer's pitch lifts — heavy churn easing into a light, airy whir. A beat.]

**GISELLE:** [quietly, almost to herself, none of the usual venom] …And there it is.

**STACEY:** There it is. By the fourth egg you'll stop doubting me. Smooth, glossy, three times the size it started. An hour ago this was groceries. Butter and four eggs. Now it could pass for something a restaurant charges you eleven dollars for, plus a small piece of your dignity. I've made it a hundred times and it still feels like a magic trick I'm getting away with. *That's* silk. You made that.

[Sound: the mixer clicks off. A half-second of quiet — then nails, clicking closer, fast.]

**STACEY:** [laughing] And the instant the loud part ends, the supervisor relocates. Straight to the bowl. Subtle as ever.

**GISELLE:** I am conducting a quality inspection. From here. With my nose. Very nearly *in* it.

**STACEY:** You are not getting raw silk filling, Your Majesty.

**GISELLE:** We will be revisiting that.

**STACEY:** Pour it into your cooled crust — watch how it settles, it almost levels itself. And then resist the spatula — you'll want to help; it does not need your help. For once in this kitchen, the move is to do nothing.

Then the hard part: you chill it. Two hours minimum — overnight if you can stand it. I know — you just want to see it. Wait anyway. This is when it sets into the real thing.

While it chills, whip the heavy cream with a little powdered sugar. You want soft peaks — they hold their shape, but the tips still flop over when you lift the whisk. Stop there. Go much past that and it overworks fast —

**GISELLE:** — and no one, *no one*, sends me a photograph of overwhipped cream gone to cottage cheese. I have a cushion. I have a kingdom. I have standards.

**STACEY:** She hasn't budged from that bowl since the mixer cut out. Not subtle, my inspector.

**STACEY:** Spread it over the chilled pie, thick. Chocolate curls on top if you're feeling fancy — and after all that, you've earned fancy. Then you slice it, and you watch whoever's at your table go quiet for a second on that first bite.

And if your first one comes out a little soft, or your curls look more like chocolate confetti — you still made this. You stood at a counter for twenty minutes betting something good was coming. It was.

**GISELLE:** [two deliberate pokes to Stacey's shin — then sits, ramrod-straight and expectant]

**STACEY:** …Did you just offer me a *sit?* For a bribe?

**GISELLE:** I offered you a transaction. One lick of cream. I supervised an entire pie — start to finish, including the parts you got wrong. The Crown takes its tribute.

**STACEY:** [laughing] One lick. And you're not even a little sorry.

**GISELLE:** The Crown is never sorry. [grandly] Kingdom conquered. Subjects fed. You may tell them it was magic — or superior instruction-following. I will allow either.

**STACEY:** Go on — go make yours. We'll be down here, negotiating the cream.

[Sound: a drawer sliding shut, the fridge door — and the patient, expectant click of nails trailing the cream bowl across the kitchen.]
