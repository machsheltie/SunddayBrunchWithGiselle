# The Pie That Started It All

*Temporary blog headnote / recipe story for French Silk Pie — our first recipe. Stacey-led, with Giselle interjections. Draft for review — not canonical content.*

---

The first French Silk Pie I ever made was a bribe.

I was twenty-something and dating someone whose family did Thanksgiving at full volume, the kind of people who laugh over each other and lob teasing across the room like a sport. I love a loud, warm kitchen now. Back then it flattened me. I'd stand at the edge of the room forgetting my own punchlines, going quiet in exactly the moments I wanted to be charming. So I gave up on talking my way into that family and decided to bake my way in instead. If I couldn't be the funny one, I'd be the one who showed up with something unforgettable.

What followed was a week my dogs still haven't forgiven. Five recipes lined up on the counter, cookbooks splayed open, sticky notes everywhere in my own increasingly unhinged handwriting: *beat the eggs LONGER. more butter. why grainy??* Giselle supervised from her cushion with the expression of someone watching a stranger redecorate her home.

> **Giselle:** *"Because normal people simply choose a recipe and bake it. How refreshingly uncomplicated."*

Here's what I wish someone had told nervous-me, so you don't panic the way I did: this pie is almost entirely patience. You beat the butter and sugar, drizzle in the cooled chocolate, then add the eggs one at a time and keep beating, a full five minutes after each one. Somewhere around minute three you will be certain it's broken and grainy and you've wasted two sticks of good butter. Keep going. That ridiculous, almost meditative beating is the entire trick. It's what turns plain ingredients into something that actually earns the word silk. (And yes, this version uses a box crust. Giselle calls that "an offense to her very being." I call it a Tuesday. Buy the good one and feel zero guilt about it.)

I carried that finished pie into Thanksgiving like a relic. Someone took one bite, tilted their head, and said, "it's… a bit rich." And I heard, loud and clear, *you're a bit much.* It took me an embarrassingly long time to land on the obvious: they were right that it's rich, and completely wrong about what that meant. Some people taste all of that and want it toned down. Some of us taste it and say *more, please.* This is a pie for the second kind of person.

> **Giselle:** *"The right people see thoughtfulness where others see extravagance."* (And then, with none of her usual venom:) *"Those are the only people worth baking for anyway."*

That "too rich" pie became the one people now request for their birthdays. Eventually it became the reason there's a podcast, a website, and four very opinionated Shelties weighing in on your dessert. So this is where we begin: the first recipe, the pie that started everything, made for anyone who's ever been told they're too much by people who just wanted easy. Pour something warm, give it a slow afternoon, and pull up a chair at the table. I'm really glad you're here.
