# Recipe Page Segments — The Sunday Brunch Format

*A repeatable guide for writing every recipe page on the site so the Shelties sound like themselves every single time. Stacey narrates and holds all authority; each dog gets one segment matched to their podcast role. Companion to the individual persona files in this folder.*

> **Before you write: read `Personas/CANON-INDEX.md`** — the canon map + preflight reading (characters, timeline, writing-issues, the read-only records). It routes you to everything this guide assumes you know.

---

## The idea in one line

Every recipe page is a little episode. Stacey tells the story and runs the kitchen; Havok, Tiana, and Phaedra each get one short, skippable box matched to their specialty; Giselle bookends the whole thing. Same four spots, every recipe, so readers learn the rhythm and start looking forward to it.

---

## Global rules (these protect the brand — don't break them)

1. **The dogs never interrupt the actual cooking.** Every character bit is a set-apart box placed *around* the recipe, never inside a numbered step, a measurement, a timer, or a warning. A reader who only wants the recipe should be able to glide right past every box.
2. **Safety and truth belong to Stacey's human voice.** Food safety (raw eggs, allergens, temperatures), real memories about real people or the real dogs, grief, consent, corrections, and the final word on the recipe are *always* Stacey. The dogs may celebrate, explain, or joke around these things, but they are never the authority on them.
3. **Each dog appears once per page.** Giselle is the exception: she opens (in the story) and closes (the verdict). Nobody else doubles up.
4. **Every box must earn its spot.** One clear purpose tied to that dog's specialty, useful to someone making this for the first time, and understandable with zero inside knowledge. If a box is only a joke and teaches nothing, cut it or fix it.
5. **Whimsy is seasoning, not the meal.** Keep every box short (lengths below). Four long character boxes will bury the recipe and lose the casual baker. Short and sparkly beats long and clever.
6. **If a dog states a fact, it has to be true.** Especially Havok's substitutions and Phaedra's science. Anything untested gets flagged honestly (Havok can do this in-character: "unconfirmed intel").
7. **Header format is consistent:** a paw, the segment name, then a short italic purpose tag. Example: `🐾 Tiana's Tasting Notes — what a perfect slice is like`.

---

## The page map (order top to bottom)

1. **The Story** — headnote *(Stacey, with Giselle interjecting)*
2. Title · one-line description · stats line *(Stacey)*
3. **Read This First** — the 2–3 things that decide success, incl. any safety note *(Stacey)*
4. Equipment · Ingredients
5. **🐾 Havok's Kitchen Recon** — gear + substitution intel
6. Instructions, numbered, with "what you're watching for" cues *(Stacey)*
7. **🐾 Tiana's Tasting Notes** — the finished slice
8. Notes: any safety/cooked-method note *(Stacey)* → **🐾 Phaedra's Porch Light** → make-ahead & storage *(Stacey)*
9. **🐾 Giselle's Grand Verdict** — the closing decree

# The segments

## 1. The Story (Stacey, with Giselle interjecting)

**Essence — Stacey:** warm, socially anxious, neurodivergent, nostalgic. A compassionate overthinker who talks to you like a friend on the phone while she bakes, rambles a little about why a recipe matters, and catches herself with a self-deprecating aside. **Giselle here:** the regal sidekick who breaks in with one or two sharp, loving jabs.

**Job:** Make a stranger want to stay and join this world. Give the dish a reason to exist that's personal and specific — a memory, a stumble, a why-this-one.

**Must include:**

- A real hook in the first line or two (a confession or a strong opinion, not a warm-up).
- A specific personal thread (a memory, a moment, a feeling), not generic "this is a family favorite."
- At least one beat that quietly sells the world: the Shelties, the Sunday-table feeling, the "too much is just enough" ethos — shown, never explained.
- 2–3 Giselle interjections, clearly set off.
- A closing line that invites the reader in and points forward.

**Length:** 4–6 short paragraphs, roughly 250–400 words. Giselle's interjections are 1–2 sentences each.

**Voice kit — Stacey:** "Hi friend." · talks to the dogs mid-thought · self-deprecating tangents she pulls herself out of · shows the anxiety instead of labeling it · "I'm so glad you're here." **Voice kit — Giselle:** see segment 5.

**Avoid:** opening with a setup frame ("I should tell you…"), moralizing or a self-help speech, listing the recipe's virtues like ad copy.

**Blind-test tell:** Stacey sounds like a real, slightly nervous person being honest with you; the line carries personal accountability that only a human could own.

---

## 2. 🐾 Havok's Kitchen Recon — *equipment & substitution intel*

**Essence:** the self-appointed war correspondent of the kitchen. Deadpan military jargon applied to completely ordinary gear and swaps. Overconfident, theatrical, lovable, occasionally accidentally wise. Think Sgt. Bilko / Hawkeye / Bill Murray in *Stripes*.

**Job:** Tell the reader what equipment they actually need and what they can safely swap — *before* they start — so nobody gets halfway in and panics. This is the practical "can I make this with what I have?" box.

**Must include:**

- A **gear check**: the one tool that matters most and the honest workaround (e.g., stand vs. hand mixer).
- **Substitution intel** with confidence levels: what swaps are safe, and a clear *do-not-swap* on anything that would actually wreck the recipe.
- Anything risky or untested deferred to "Command" (Stacey) — in character.

**Length:** 60–110 words. Two short briefings (Gear, then Subs). Tight — he reports, he doesn't ramble.

**Voice kit:** "Agent Havok reporting from Kitchen Base Alpha." · "Mission:" / "Phase two:" / "Recon active." · names objects as adversaries or assets ("the Mixer Beast," "rogue dish towel") · "Mission rating: 10/10. Would deploy again." · "failure is just success that hasn't been reported yet." · defers to "Command."

**Avoid:** giving a substitution that isn't actually safe just because it's funny; encouraging any unsafe shortcut; burying the real answer under so much jargon the reader can't find it. The intel has to be findable and correct.

**Blind-test tell:** a line reads like a battlefield dispatch about butter wrappers — serious tone, absurd subject, real information underneath.

---

## 3. 🐾 Tiana's Tasting Notes — *what a perfect slice is like*

**Essence:** the joyful, dreamy, exuberant taster. Luna Lovegood meets Pinkie Pie. Hyper-specific sensory delight, rapid happy bursts, describes how the food *feels* and the joy it brings, not just how it tastes.

**Job:** Two things at once — make the reader hungry, and quietly show them what "done right" looks, feels, and sounds like (so it doubles as a doneness cue). The reward at the end of the steps.

**Must include:**

- The first-bite moment: texture, temperature, the sound, the way it behaves on the fork.
- At least one cue that signals success without sounding like an instruction (e.g., "stands tall on the fork, then melts" = properly set).
- Her joy and a feeling, not just flavor.

**Length:** 50–85 words. One breathless paragraph. Resist the urge to let her run long — her magic is in short, sparkly bursts.

**Voice kit:** "Oh, hello, hello, HELLO!" · "dances on your tongue like…" · "it's not a bully" · "wants to be friends with your taste buds" · "makes my tail wag so fast it could power a small village!" · "Five paws up!" · "Hooray for pie!" · sound effects (*snap!*).

**Avoid:** turning shrill or hyperactive; vague praise ("so good!") with no sensory specifics; so much sparkle that the doneness cue gets lost.

**Blind-test tell:** every sensation becomes a tiny magical character or event, and her tail/heart is involved.

---

## 4. 🐾 Phaedra's Porch Light — *why it works, and how to fix it if it doesn't*

**Essence:** the resident science dogter. Princess Twilight Sparkle warmth — organized, curious, gently enthusiastic, never condescending. Believes understanding the "why" makes baking more magical, not less, and that the why is what lets you fix things.

**Job:** Explain the one piece of science that makes this recipe work, then give the reader the troubleshooting fixes so they can rescue it. This is the confidence-builder.

**Must include:**

- One "why it works" explanation tied to *this* recipe's key step (kept simple, connected to a practical payoff).
- 3–5 troubleshooting fixes in symptom → cause → fix form.
- A warm, capable send-off.
- **Never** the safety authority — she explains technique and recovery; Stacey owns the safety/egg/allergen call. Phaedra can explain *why* the safe method works, but the directive stays Stacey's.

**Length:** 80–140 words. One short why-it-works paragraph + a tight fix list.

**Voice kit:** "Here's something wonderful…" · "The beautiful thing about…" · "Let me explain what's happening…" · connects chemistry to a benefit ("…so the filling turns satin-smooth instead of gritty") · "You become a kitchen scientist." · "You've got this."

**Avoid:** jargon dumps with no payoff; a lecturing tone; taking over the safety warning; fixes that are vague ("just keep trying").

**Blind-test tell:** the science is framed as a delightful little secret she can't wait to share, and it always ends by making the reader feel more capable.

---

## 5. 🐾 Giselle's Grand Verdict — *the closing decree*

**Essence:** the queen. Sharp, witty, mock-formal, Devil-Wears-Prada-meets-Queen-of-Hearts, with deep protectiveness under the sass. She is quality control, and this is her courtroom.

**Job:** Deliver the final word — a verdict that crowns the recipe and lands the brand's "be unapologetically extra" note. She also opened the page in the story, so this closes her arc.

**Must include:**

- A quick indictment of the lazy version (shortcuts, pre-fab, "disappointment wearing a wig").
- A verdict in her favor on *this* recipe ("not guilty" / "it is, in fact, correct").
- A closing flourish that ties to being worth the effort. Often: "Just like me."

**Length:** 25–55 words. Two to four sentences. A decree, not a paragraph.

**Voice kit:** "Let the record show…" · "I decree…" · "Darling…" · "I have standards." · "You are not a baker; you are an assembler." · "It is not guilty. It is, in fact, correct." · "Just like me."

**Avoid:** real cruelty (she targets choices and shortcuts, never vulnerable people); going soft and losing the bite; running long — the power is in the clipped finality.

**Blind-test tell:** it reads like a royal ruling handed down with a raised eyebrow, sharp on the surface and protective underneath.

---

## Stacey — the voice that holds it all together

Not a box; she's the whole page. Stacey writes the story, the description, Read This First, every instruction, every safety note, make-ahead, and storage. Warm, conversational, encouraging, a little self-aware, talking to one friend. She is the only voice for: **food safety, allergens, raw-egg/temperature calls, real memories, corrections, and the final recipe word.** The dogs add delight on top of her kitchen; they never run it.

# Worked example — French Silk Pie (the test drive)

*The five segments below were written straight from this guide. Full headnote story lives in `docs/editorial-drafts/recipes/1_french_silk_pie/french-silk-pie-blog-story-DRAFT.md`; the four page boxes are the live versions from `docs/editorial-drafts/recipes/1_french_silk_pie/french-silk-pie-recipe-DRAFT.md`.*

**The Story (Stacey + Giselle) — opening line:** *"The first French Silk Pie I ever made was a bribe."* (Full 6-paragraph headnote in the story draft: the loud-Thanksgiving "bake my way in" story, the "it's a bit rich / you're a bit much" beat, the reframe, and the close that names the podcast, the site, and the four Shelties. Giselle interjects twice.)

**🐾 Havok's Kitchen Recon** — *equipment & substitution intel; skip if your gear's squared away* (94 words)

> Agent Havok reporting from Kitchen Base Alpha. Pre-mission briefing as follows.
>
> **Gear check.** The Mixer Beast is your primary weapon. Stand mixer strongly advised — this is a twenty-minute engagement, and with a hand mixer, *your arm* is the stand. Still wins the day; just rotate troops.
>
> **Substitution intel, confidence levels attached.** Crust is dealer's choice: store-bought, homemade, or crushed-cookie all hold the position. Chips on top are decorative — semisweet, dark, or white all deploy fine. But do *not* swap the unsweetened baking chocolate for sweet chocolate without clearance from Command (Stacey); it wrecks the sugar balance. Failure is just success that hasn't been reported yet.

**🐾 Tiana's Tasting Notes** — *what a perfect slice is like* (78 words)

> Oh, hello, hello, HELLO — okay, the first forkful! It doesn't crunch and it doesn't squish, it just *melts*, cool and silky, like a little chocolate cloud that learned some manners. The chocolate is deep and rich but it is NOT a bully; it only wants to be friends with your whole mouth. Then the whipped cream floats in soft and barely-sweet, and the shards go *snap!* A perfect slice stands tall on the fork, then disappears. Five paws up. Hooray for pie!

**🐾 Phaedra's Porch Light** — *why it works, and how to fix it if it doesn't* (126 words)

> Here's something wonderful: that long beat is doing two invisible jobs at once. It slowly dissolves the sugar so the filling turns satin-smooth instead of gritty, and it whisks in thousands of tiny air bubbles that give the pie its light, mousse-like body. That's the whole difference between "fine" and "silk."
>
> If something looks off, there's almost always a temperature reason and a gentle fix:
>
> - *Soupy filling?* Your chocolate or butter was still warm. Chill the bowl 15 minutes and re-whip.
> - *Grainy or gritty?* The sugar hasn't dissolved yet. Keep beating — you can't overdo it.
> - *Cream won't hold peaks?* The cream or bowl is too warm; cold-and-cold whips beautifully.
>
> Understand the why, and you can rescue almost anything. You've got this.

**🐾 Giselle's Grand Verdict** (38 words)

> *"Let the record show: this pie does not shy away from the butter or the chocolate. It whispers, 'yes, I'm extra, and worth every calorie.' It is not guilty. It is, in fact, correct."*

# On the website (how these render)

The segments are not interjected inside the steps and not piled into one block — they are distinct, skippable cards anchored at meaningful points in the reading flow. All four use the existing `SheltieTip` component (`sunday-brunch-website/src/components/SheltieTip.jsx`).

**Anchor positions in `RecipeTemplate.jsx`:**

- Giselle interjections — inside the `story` block (prose, not a card).
- **Havok's Kitchen Recon** — before the ingredients/steps grid (pre-bake briefing).
- *(steps grid stays clean — no cards inside it.)*
- **Tiana's Tasting Notes** — just after the steps.
- **Phaedra's Porch Light** — below the grid.
- **Giselle's Grand Verdict** — at the close, by the guestbook.

**Component usage.** `SheltieTip` takes `character`, an optional `segmentName` (the formal title, shown with a 🐾), and an optional `purpose` (the one-line tag). Without `segmentName` it stays a casual tip ("Havok barks…"). It renders as an accessible `<aside>` with an `aria-label` of the segment name.

**Data shape.** Segment copy is authored per recipe on `recipe.shelties` (each value a string or array of paragraphs); the template renders whatever is present and falls back to the default example tips when a recipe omits them:

```js
shelties: {
    havok: "…",            // string or [paragraphs]
    tiana: "…",
    phaedra: ["…", "…"],
    giselleVerdict: "…"
}
```

**Rules baked into the implementation:**

- **Skippable + print-clean.** `@media print { .sheltie-tip { display: none } }` keeps the dogs out of the printed/copied recipe.
- **Accessibility (WCAG AA).** Each card is an `<aside>` with a label, outside the steps `<ol>`; avatars carry the dog's name as alt text.
- **Consistency.** Same anchors on every recipe, so readers learn the rhythm.
- **Length.** Keep each card to the word counts above; four long cards bury the recipe.
